Lab Overview

Read this first so your team understands the challenge, rules, and success criteria.

Essential Question

How can multiple teams use the law of reflection to accurately guide a laser beam across a building?

Learning Targets

I can apply the law of reflection to predict light paths.
I can create and use a scale map for a real space.
I can design and build a stable mirror support system.
I can communicate beam location and direction to another team.
I can analyze why actual results may differ from predictions.

Safety Rules

Never point a laser at a person’s eyes.
Do not look directly into the beam or a bright reflection.
Keep the beam within the teacher-approved height range.
No holding mirrors by hand during the final run.
Teacher approval is required before full-system testing.

Key Physics Idea

Law of Reflection: The angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection. Both angles are measured from the normal line, not from the mirror surface.

Use this space in class to sketch a mirror, a normal line, an incoming ray, and a reflected ray.
Tip: Label the normal, angle of incidence, and angle of reflection clearly.

Success Criteria

  • Your team accurately maps its hallway or corridor.
  • Your team predicts a usable beam path through its section.
  • Your mirror system is stable and adjustable.
  • Your team communicates the output beam clearly to the next team.
  • Your team documents revisions and explains errors.

Important: Your team is not only responsible for your own section. You are responsible for making your section work with the entire class relay.

Helpful Reference Panel

Keep these reminders in mind while you work.

What should a good corridor plan include?

A strong plan includes a clear entry point, an exit point, realistic mirror locations, a scale map, and a beam path that avoids obstacles while still being adjustable during testing.

Why do we draw a normal line?

The normal line is the reference line used to measure the angle of incidence and the angle of reflection. If you measure from the mirror surface instead, your angles will be wrong.

What if our first design does not work?

That is part of engineering. Record the problem, make a controlled change, test again, and explain why the redesign improved the result.

How can teams work better together?

Use exact language when describing the beam: location, direction, and height. Avoid vague directions like “move it a little left.”
